# Double-Helix Vision (DH)

> **A geometry-based visual sampler that compresses 2D images into 1D signals using spiral trajectories — designed for extreme efficiency in bandwidth-constrained environments.**

[![Open In Colab](https://colab.research.google.com/assets/colab-badge.svg)](https://colab.research.google.com/github/JackJ-C/double-helix-vision-tool/blob/main/DH.ipynb)

## 💡 The Concept

DH addresses a specific problem: **How can AI agents "see" effectively when bandwidth or compute is extremely limited?**

Instead of processing every pixel of a 1080p frame, DH uses a pair of **Golden Spirals** (Alpha & Beta, phase-shifted by 180°) to sample the image. This creates a biologically-inspired foveated sampling pattern — high density at the center (Fovea), low density at the periphery.

![DH Concept](assets/What.png)
*(Figure: The forward path compresses 2D space into a 1D signal via dual-spiral geometry. The signal converges at the Singularity point.)*

### What DH Does

- **Forward Scan (2D → 1D):** Collapses a full image into two compact 1D signal streams (~2,894 points per helix) by sampling along spiral trajectories.
- **Reflection Engine (1D → 2D → 3D):** Reverses the process — projects 1D signals back through the spiral to reconstruct spatial structure, enabling motion detection and depth estimation without neural networks.
- **Robotics API:** Provides a JSON-serializable spatial perception interface with sub-millisecond latency, ready for LLM-driven or traditional robot control systems.

### 3. Motion Detection — Temporal Collision

DH detects motion by comparing consecutive 1D signals (frame-differencing in the collapsed domain). Tested with synthetic scenes containing objects moving at known speeds:

| Object Speed | Mean Collision | Max Collision | Correlation (r) |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| 0.5 (slow) | 0.184 | 0.674 | 0.29 |
| 1.0 | 0.216 | 0.978 | 0.62 |
| 2.0 | 0.475 | 1.406 | 0.62 |
| 5.0 (fast) | 1.002 | 3.084 | **0.75** |
| 10.0 (very fast) | 1.325 | 3.878 | 0.67 |

Collision intensity scales with object speed (r ≈ 0.59–0.75). The correlation is moderate — this is expected, as spiral sampling is foveated (center-biased), so objects outside the foveal region produce weaker signals. In real-world video tests, where the entire scene shifts between frames, the collision signal is significantly more pronounced.

## 🧠 Design Philosophy

**Why dual spirals? Why foveated sampling?**

* **The Baseline Problem:** Random or Grid sampling treats all pixels as equally important. This wastes bandwidth on background noise.
* **The DH Approach:** In robotics and active perception, an agent needs to **"lock on"** to a target in the center of its field of view, not uniformly scan the scenery.
  * Alpha & Beta helices (180° phase-shifted) act like two "eyes," providing natural stereo disparity at every depth layer.
  * The foveal concentration means DH captures **more information per sample point** in the region that matters most for decision-making.

**In short:** DH is not an image compressor — it is a **biologically-inspired spatial encoder** that converts 2D visual scenes into compact 1D signals for downstream reasoning.

# Get JSON-ready spatial perception report
report = api.process_frame(frame)

## ⚠️ Known Limitations

- **Not a depth sensor.** DH estimates relative spatial structure through stereo disparity and temporal collision. It does not provide absolute distance measurements (e.g., "3.2 meters") without external calibration.
- **Foveal bias.** Objects outside the central foveal region receive fewer sample points and may be missed. This is by design, but limits peripheral detection.
- **Overexposure failure.** In scenes with pixel saturation (brightness ≥ 245), all contrast is lost and motion detection fails. This is a fundamental limitation of passive vision.
- **Single-frame depth.** Stereo depth from Alpha-Beta disparity provides structural cues but is weaker than physical dual-camera setups. Temporal collision (multi-frame) gives stronger depth signals.
